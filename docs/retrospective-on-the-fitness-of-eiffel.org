#+TITLE: Retrospective on the Fitness of Eiffel for RESTLY
#+AUTHOR: Alejandro Garcia
#+DATE: 2026-07-14
#+STARTUP: overview

* Purpose

After building RESTLY (core combinators, the EWF bridge, the reflective converter, and the SQLite-backed todobackend example), we paused to ask two questions.
Which parts of the implementation did Eiffel actively help?
Where did we fight the language, or its ecosystem, more than it helped us?
The answers close with a list of the attributes an ideal language for RESTLY would have.

* What worked — the language pulled its weight

** Contracts as the HTTP layer

The =error_NNN_= assertion-label grammar (parsed by =status_from_tag= in {RESTLY_EWF_CONTRACT_GUARD}) is the most Eiffel-native idea in the repo.
A REST 4xx is literally "the client violated a precondition", so rescue/retry plus tag parsing turns Design by Contract into the error-handling layer for free.
No exception hierarchy, no error-code enum.
This would not work in any language where assertions are debug-only.

** Verbs as types

{RESTLY_LISTABLE}, {RESTLY_POSTABLE} and {RESTLY_PATCHABLE} with =attached {RESTLY_POSTABLE [...]} storage as l_post= make capability negotiation a conformance test.
A store picks its verbs by inheritance; an unsupported route falls out as 405 naturally.
Multiple inheritance used for exactly what it is good at.

** The mismatch ledger

{TODOBACKEND_CONVERTER_JSON_OBJECT_TODO_ROW} is three declarations; everything else flows by reflection, and =check_schema= fails at wiring time, not mid-request.
That is the {MISMATCH_CORRECTOR} idea paying rent.
Void safety helped everywhere here too — a whole category of =/= Void= contracts simply does not exist.

** Small comforts that added up

Convert clauses (=l_key := "foo"= for {JSON_STRING}), inline =create {CLASS}= expressions, and =once ("PROCESS")= wiring.
The slimmed {TODOBACKEND_SQLITE_EXECUTION} reads like a wiring diagram.

* Where we fought — mostly the ecosystem, twice the language

** The language itself, genuinely

The headline is the generics ceiling.
Eiffel has generics ({RESTLY_CONVERTER} [R, S] is one), but four specific limitations hit us, and all four land on encode/decode:

1. No feature-level type parameters.
   A feature cannot introduce its own generic — only the class can.
   There is no =decode [T] (a_json): T=; every (R, S) pairing must be a /class/.
   That, more than VHPR(5), is why the converter hierarchy kept sprouting named classes — the "five inheritance classes is disappointing" moment.
2. VHPR(5).
   No two generic derivations of the same parent — it closed the mixin design, closed the convert-clause approaches, and means one converter can never speak two formats ({RESTLY_FORMAT} [JSON_OBJECT] and a hypothetical [XML_DOC] cannot coexist as parents).
   We paid the class count down to four, but the floor is set by the rule, not the design.
3. No ad-hoc polymorphism (type classes, overloading, specialization).
   You cannot dispatch on a type to pick its codec, so the engine dispatches at runtime on =field_type (i)= constants — the =inspect= blocks in =to_store= / =to_representation= are hand-written what serde derives.
4. Constrained genericity too weak to see inside S.
   =S -> ANY= says nothing about fields, so schema agreement cannot be a compile-time fact; =check_schema= at wiring time is the best available approximation.

One line: Eiffel can only /interpret/ a schema at runtime; it can never /derive/ from one.
serde, Pydantic and kotlinx all derive.

A fifth, separate fight: no metadata on attributes.
Renames, skips and coercions belong /on the attribute/ (an annotation), but Eiffel has no such channel, so =correct_mismatches= exists as a parallel ledger the schema check must reconcile.
The wiring-time check is us compensating for what the type system cannot say.

** The ecosystem, repeatedly

- ABEL's relational connector: no BOOLEAN round-trip (so =TODO_ROW.completed: INTEGER= — persistence leaking into the domain class, patched back by a coercion), no DDL generation (hand-written CREATE TABLE), and not thread-safe under EWF's threads — the open catcall bug that caps the acceptance suite at 7/16.
- The bundled JSON serializer: rejected outright — character fields dropped, negative REAL_64 mangled, zero hooks.
  We rebuilt that wheel because the shipped one was square.
- Concurrency defaults: =support="none"= silently breaking libcurl; SCOOP-era libraries meeting a threaded server.
  Every concurrency lesson was learned by a wedged process, not a compile error.
- Tooling: AutoTest cannot filter one test; new classes need clean compiles; the string zoo (=to_string_8= sprinkled through the handler) is a friction tax on every boundary.

** Honest summary

The design vocabulary of Eiffel — contracts, conformance, multiple inheritance, void safety — matched REST unusually well; those parts feel discovered, not built.
The fights concentrate at the edges: the generics ceiling (four limitations, all landing on encode/decode), one missing channel (attribute metadata), and a library ecosystem that has not been hardened by the traffic RESTLY puts through it.
The unfixed SQLite threading bug is the truest symbol of the ecosystem half: our code is fine, the layer under it is not.

And "at the edges" is not a small place.
Fielding's uniform interface says resources are manipulated /only through representations/ — the Representation ↔ Store conversion (JSON in, SQLite down, and back) is one of REST's two load-bearing walls, contracts being the other.
So the harsher verdict: Eiffel is the strongest language on one load-bearing wall of REST and among the weakest on the other.
The mismatch ledger and reflective engine we are proud of are good masonry on a wall the language refused to hold up.

* The fourteen attributes of an ideal language for RESTLY

Points 1–10 came from the first retrospective pass; points 11–14 were added after the generics-ceiling analysis sharpened what "the conversions R → S → R need to be really good" actually demands.

1. Runtime-checked Design by Contract with blame assignment and contract inheritance.
   Contract violations generate =error_4xx= and =error_500=; the language must know /who/ violated the contract, because that is the 4xx/5xx split: precondition = client's fault = 4xx, postcondition or invariant = supplier's fault = 500.
   Racket's contract system is the only one that does blame natively.
   Contract inheritance (=require else= weakening) is what lets a subresource legally accept more than its parent.
   /Runtime-checked/ is load-bearing (lesson from evaluating Prusti): a trust boundary needs checks that fire on untrusted input, not proofs that exclude violations at compile time — static and runtime contracts are complements, not substitutes.
2. A pipe operator.
   The combinator chain (cache → forwarder → table) /is/ a pipeline; we simulate it with constructor nesting in =todo_pipeline=.
3. Best-in-class bidirectional data transformation: JSON → domain → database and back.
   With the lesson from =check_schema= baked in: mismatch declarations (rename, skip, coerce) must be verified against the schema at wiring time, not discovered mid-request, and declared once for both directions — =convert_boolean_integer_field= takes both lambdas together for a reason.
4. Verbs as types with runtime capability query.
   =attached {RESTLY_POSTABLE} storage= is the whole routing story: a store /is/ its verb set, and unsupported = 405 falls out of a failed conformance test.
   Needs interfaces or traits plus safe downcast (Rust deliberately makes this painful; Eiffel, Kotlin and C# shine).
5. A metadata channel on fields.
   Annotations or decorators, so the mismatch ledger lives on the attribute instead of in a parallel =correct_mismatches= hook that a schema check must reconcile.
   The single thing Eiffel most lacks.
6. Null/void safety.
   Deleted a whole category of contracts and checks for us; non-negotiable.
7. Compiler-checked concurrency.
   Our only unfixed bug is a data race the compiler happily allowed (threads sharing a =once ("PROCESS")= repository, catcall at runtime).
   Ideal: sharing violations are compile errors (Rust's Send/Sync; SCOOP promised this and the ecosystem abandoned it).
8. No runtime type holes.
   The catcall /was/ Eiffel's covariance unsoundness surfacing in production.
   Ideal language: variance checked at compile time.
9. Instance-free type introspection, ideally at compile time.
   The reflective converter needed a fresh instance just to read the schema; =serde=-style derive does it at zero runtime cost and would also generate our DDL.
10. Boring, hardened libraries.
    Not a language feature, but the honest retrospective says ABEL and the JSON serializer cost more than every language limitation combined.
    An ideal language has a dull, battle-tested HTTP server and DB driver.
11. Feature-level generic parameters.
    =decode [T] (input): T= as a /feature/, not a class; in Eiffel every (R, S) pairing costs a named class, so the domain converter is a tax, not a choice.
    (We happen to like the named class for readability — the ideal language makes it optional, not mandatory.)
12. Schema agreement as a compile-time fact.
    The compiler can see the fields of both R and S, derive the codec and the DDL, and reject a rename typo or type mismatch at compile time.
    =check_schema= at wiring time is the runtime consolation prize; derivation over interpretation.
13. Multi-format coexistence.
    One program — even one converter — speaks JSON, YAML and XML side by side; the format is a plugged-in value, not an inheritance decision.
    VHPR(5) made it an inheritance decision, so an Eiffel converter can never speak two formats.
14. Round-trip laws as checkable contracts.
    =to_representation (to_store (r)) ≍ r= stated once per converter and enforced by property test or proof.
    RESTLY's PATCH already bets on this law — RFC 7386 merge works by converter round-trip in {RESTLY_JSON_PIPELINE_FRONT} — but nothing checks it today.

Prusti (Rust + static contracts) has 3 + 5 + 6 + 7 + 8 + 9, halves of 1 and 10 — numerically the best column yet, but the half of 1 it lacks is the half RESTLY is about.
Ada (with SPARK) has 1 + 4 + 7 + 8, halves of 6 and 10 — arguably the strongest 1 on the board, but misses the whole metaprogramming column (3, 5, 9).
Python with Pydantic + SQLModel has 3 + 4 + 5 + 9 + 10, halves of 1 and 6 — the best data column on the board, with almost none of the safety column.

The Rebol family (Rye, Arturo, Red) tops out at Rye's 2 + 5 + 9 with halves of 1, 3, 4 and 6 — the best pipe story and the only built-in validation dialect on the board, and no compile time at all.

On the conversion points 11–14 (added later, not folded into the rows above):
Rust/serde has 11 + 12 + 13 (12 is its crown; 14 via proptest, tested not proven).
Kotlin has 11 + 12 + 13 and half of 14 (kotest property tests; =reified= type parameters even survive erasure at the call site).
D has 11 + 12 + 13 and half of 14 (built-in =unittest= blocks make the law cheap to state) — Nim's twin on the conversion column.
Python/Pydantic has 11 + 13, and 12 at class-definition time rather than compile time.
Rhombus has 11 + 13, and 12 at macro-expansion time.
Ada has none of 11–14.

Nim has 11 + 12 + 13 and half of 14 — the entire conversion column, tied with Rust/serde and Kotlin.
The Rebol family has 11 and 13 in the runtime sense (dynamic languages get them vacuously) and none of 12 or 14 — interpretation perfected, derivation impossible.
Eiffel has only half of 14 — the round-trip law is /statable/ as a postcondition, the one conversion point contracts give us — and none of 11–13.
Nobody has all fourteen — which is roughly why RESTLY is interesting research.

* Conclusion — should RESTLY stay in Eiffel?

Yes — because RESTLY's thesis is only expressible in three languages, and Eiffel is the best of them.

** The thesis has exactly three possible homes

RESTLY's interesting claim is /contracts as the HTTP layer/:
blame → 4xx/500, =require else= as subresource weakening, verbs as types.
That needs runtime DbC with contract inheritance, which exists in Eiffel, Ada, and Racket/Rhombus.
Every other candidate — including Kotlin with its widest coverage — makes the thesis a convention, not a contract.
Switching to Kotlin would not be porting RESTLY;
it would be abandoning the research to write a nice REST library that already exists five times over.
Ada loses the entire metaprogramming column harder than Eiffel does.
Rhombus is the only upgrade on the thesis axis (real blame, macros as the metadata channel) —
at the price of every static guarantee.

** The wall Eiffel fails is mechanical; the wall it holds is conceptual

The conversion boilerplate is a tax, but it is boring tax — the mismatch ledger works, it is just verbose.
And the retrospective undersold one thing:
this project already prefers named per-domain converter classes as a matter of style.
Point 11 calls the named class a tax; our own style calls it a choice we would make anyway.
What Eiffel cannot do — derive the internals — is exactly what code generation is good at.
"Derive outside the compiler" is an honest workaround for the interpret-vs-derive ceiling;
serde is codegen too, it just lives inside the build.

** The graveyard is a warning about switching, not staying

Three attempts to have both walls died (C# Code Contracts, Cobra, D's contracts)
because contracts were not load-bearing for anything.
In Eiffel they are load-bearing for the whole culture — the standard library keeps them honest.
Leaving Eiffel means leaving the one place the thesis is native.

** The caveat that would change the answer

If the goal shifts from research ("can contracts be the error layer?")
to product ("a todobackend people deploy"),
then Kotlin, without hesitation:
folk-RESTLY via =require()= → 400 plus kotlinx.serialization ships in a weekend and stands on the JVM.
And if a second implementation ever happens as research — the comparison paper writes itself —
Rhombus is the counterpart that keeps the thesis and fixes both of our genuine language fights.

** Verdict

Eiffel for RESTLY-the-thesis, with generated converters as the prosthetic for the data wall.
The two-walls verdict stands —
but a language that is strongest on the wall that makes the work /novel/
beats nine languages that are strong on the wall that makes it /convenient/.

* Appendix: Rhombus in detail

Rhombus (https://rhombus-lang.org/) is the Racket team's new-syntax language on the Racket VM.
Point by point:

1. DbC with blame: its strongest card.
   =::= annotations are runtime-checked, and underneath sits Racket's contract system, the only one with real blame assignment — exactly the client-fault = 4xx / supplier-fault = 500 split we want.
   Caveat: Rhombus's native annotation errors are simpler than full =racket/contract= blame; you would reach into the Racket layer for the good stuff.
   Contract inheritance à la =require else= is not a language construct; you would encode it.
2. Pipe operator: not in core, but user-defined operators with precedence are a headline feature; =operator (x |> f): f(x)= is one line.
3. JSON → domain → DB round-trip: thin.
   Racket's =json= and =db= libraries are solid but there is no serde/kotlinx equivalent; the reflective converter with its mismatch ledger would be ours to build again.
4. Verbs as types with runtime capability query: =interface=, =class= and =is_a= checks; the =attached {RESTLY_POSTABLE}= pattern translates directly.
5. Metadata channel on fields: the second strong card.
   Class-clause macros mean =correct_mismatches= could become declarations inside the class body, checked at expansion time.
   The macro system is the metadata channel, arbitrarily extensible — best in class.
6. Null/void safety: dynamic language; annotations catch misuse at runtime, not compile time.
7. Compiler-checked concurrency: green threads, futures, places; no race checking.
8. No runtime type holes: vacuously true — there are no static claims to violate, every check is at runtime.
   Honest, but it fails the spirit (compile-time soundness).
9. Instance-free introspection / compile-time derive: macros run at compile time; a serde-style derive (and our DDL generator) is writable and idiomatic, but nothing exists off the shelf.
10. Boring, hardened libraries: Racket's web-server and =db= are 20+ years old and dull in the good way — more hardened than ABEL ever was — but the ecosystem is Eiffel-sized: one maintainer per library.

Verdict: RESTLY-in-Rhombus would keep the thesis (contracts → HTTP, now with real blame) and fix our two genuine language fights (VHPR-5 gone, field metadata via macros), at the price of trading compile-time guarantees for runtime ones.

* Appendix: Prusti in detail

Prusti (https://www.pm.inf.ethz.ch/research/prusti.html, ETH Zurich) is the inverse experiment: Rust with contracts checked /statically/ — Viper-based compile-time proofs lifted from Rust's ownership types.
Point by point:

1. DbC with blame: split down the middle.
   The 500 half is done better than anyone — postconditions and invariants are proven, so =error_500= is not handled, it is unreachable.
   The 4xx half is impossible by construction: RESTLY's precondition → 4xx trick depends on contract violations happening at runtime, thrown by clients you cannot verify (HTTP callers).
   Prusti's model is that code which might violate a precondition does not compile — but the HTTP boundary is not Rust code, so you are back to hand-written input validation, the exact thing DbC was saving us from.
   Blame exists, but at compile time (the failing call site), not as a runtime party you can map to a status code.
   Half of 1, maximal strength; the other half structurally excluded.
2. Pipe operator: plain Rust — no operator, method chaining approximates.
   No.
3. Data round-trip: serde, inherited from Rust.
   Caveat: Prusti verifies /your/ fragment; derive-generated and third-party code is trusted via external specs, not verified.
   Yes.
4. Verbs as types with runtime capability query: Rust's weak point stands — trait downcasting is deliberately painful, and Prusti's supported fragment handles trait objects poorly anyway.
   No.
5. Metadata channel on fields: serde attributes.
   Yes.
6. Null/void safety: =Option=.
   Yes.
7. Compiler-checked concurrency: Send/Sync, plus Prusti proofs on top.
   Yes — strongest of any candidate.
8. No runtime type holes: yes, and Prusti extends soundness beyond types to functional properties.
   Yes — strongest.
9. Compile-time derive/introspection: yes.
10. Boring, hardened libraries: Rust's ecosystem yes, but Prusti itself fails its own test — research-grade, "a small but interesting fragment of Rust without unsafe features", and a real RESTLY (async server, closures, trait objects everywhere) sits far outside what it can verify today.
    Half.

Verdict: the mirror of Rhombus.
Prusti maximizes exactly what RESTLY does not need (proving the server correct) and structurally cannot do the thing RESTLY is about (contracts as the runtime error layer for untrusted clients).
It would produce the most trustworthy 500-free todobackend ever built, with completely conventional 4xx handling.
The sharpened lesson for the list: point 1 must say /runtime-checked/ DbC — a trust boundary needs checks that fire, not proofs that exclude.
Static and runtime contracts are complements, not substitutes.

* Appendix: Ada in detail

Ada 2012+ (with SPARK as its optional static layer) is Eiffel's closest living relative.
Point by point:

1. Runtime-checked DbC: in two ways Eiffel's superior.
   (a) =Pre=/=Post=/=Type_Invariant= aspects are runtime-checked, and Ada 2012 allows raise expressions inside contracts: =Pre => Valid (X) or else raise Bad_Request= — the status mapping becomes a typed exception per code instead of our tag-parsing of assertion labels.
   (b) It is the only mainstream-adjacent language with real contract inheritance: =Pre'Class= on tagged types is or-combined down the hierarchy — that is literally =require else= weakening.
   (c) Uniquely, SPARK gives you the Prusti trick in the same language: statically prove the 500 half where feasible, leave runtime checks firing at the boundary for the 4xx half.
   Blame is the pre-vs-post distinction, same crude level as Eiffel.
   Yes — arguably the strongest 1 on the board.
2. Pipe operator: no, and no user-defined operators beyond overloading the fixed set.
   No.
3. JSON → domain → DB round-trip: GNATcoll has a JSON library; there is no serde equivalent, and stream attributes (='Read=/='Write=) only give compiler-derived binary serialization with no field metadata.
   The mismatch-ledger converter would be hand-written per type.
   No.
4. Verbs as types with runtime capability query: tagged types, interfaces (Ada 2005), membership test =Store in Postable'Class=, checked downcasts.
   Our routing pattern translates directly.
   Yes.
5. Metadata channel on fields: aspects /look/ like the channel, but user code cannot define or introspect custom aspects; no annotations, no macros.
   No.
6. Null safety: =not null access= is opt-in, and idiomatic Ada avoids access types altogether (by-value composites), which sidesteps rather than solves it.
   Half.
7. Compiler-checked concurrency: protected objects make synchronized shared state a language construct (our ABEL race would have had an obvious home), and SPARK proves absence of data races outright.
   Not Rust's ownership model, but close in practice.
   Yes with SPARK.
8. No runtime type holes: no generic variance at all (generics instantiate), tagged-type conversions checked; no catcall analogue.
   Yes.
9. Instance-free introspection / compile-time derive: essentially no reflection, no metaprogramming.
   The reflective converter is not merely awkward — it is impossible.
   No.
10. Boring, hardened libraries: AWS (Ada Web Server) is decades old and avionics-adjacent industrial; GNATcoll for DB; Alire is a real package manager now.
    Small but genuinely dull in the good way.
    Half, better than Eiffel's.

Verdict: Ada keeps the /contract/ thesis better than Eiffel itself — contract inheritance, typed raise-per-status, plus the static/runtime combination that Prusti and Eiffel each only get half of.
But it fails the entire metaprogramming column (3, 5, 9) harder than Eiffel does: we at least had {REFLECTED_REFERENCE_OBJECT}; Ada has nothing, so every converter is hand-written boilerplate.
RESTLY-in-Ada keeps the error layer and loses the reflective converter entirely.
Mirror-image of Rhombus, sibling of Eiffel.

* Appendix: Python with Pydantic + SQLModel in detail

Not a language but a stack (Python + Pydantic + SQLModel, usually under FastAPI), included because it is the best existing answer to "easiest to read, smallest to write" multi-format serialization.
One class is simultaneously the JSON schema, the YAML schema (any format that parses to dicts), the validation rules, and the SQLite table including generated DDL:

#+begin_src python
class Todo(SQLModel, table=True):
    id: int | None = Field(default=None, primary_key=True)
    title: str
    completed: bool = False
    order_value: int = Field(alias="order")   # rename_field, on the attribute
#+end_src

Point by point:

1. Runtime-checked DbC with blame: half — the client half.
   Pydantic validation fires at the trust boundary and FastAPI maps =ValidationError= to a 422 with per-field detail: our precondition → 4xx idea, productized.
   But there is no supplier side — no postconditions, no invariants, no contract inheritance.
   The 500s are back to being bugs you find at 3am.
2. Pipe operator: no.
3. Best-in-class bidirectional data transformation: yes — this is the whole point.
   =alias=, =exclude= and custom validators are the mismatch vocabulary (rename, skip, coerce) living on the field; =SQLModel.metadata.create_all= generates the CREATE TABLE we hand-wrote in =bootstrap_schema=; model configuration is checked at class-definition time, the moral equivalent of =check_schema= at wiring.
4. Verbs as types with runtime capability query: =isinstance= plus ABCs or =runtime_checkable= Protocols; trivially yes, though nothing checks it statically.
5. Metadata channel on fields: =Field(...)=, =Annotated=, decorators — best in class alongside Rhombus.
6. Null safety: =Optional= hints checked by mypy (gradual, opt-in) and by Pydantic at the boundary; the language itself still throws =NoneType= errors.
   Half.
7. Compiler-checked concurrency: no — the GIL hides some races and checks nothing.
8. No runtime type holes: no — type hints are unenforced in the core language; everything is a hole unless Pydantic guards the boundary.
9. Instance-free type introspection: =model_fields= / =get_type_hints= — runtime rather than compile time, but zero-ceremony and the entire stack is built on it.
   Yes.
10. Boring, hardened libraries: SQLAlchemy, FastAPI, requests — the dullest, most battle-tested column of any candidate.
    Yes.
    Caveat: TypeDB (or any non-relational target) is manual in every language; no serializer derives TypeQL.

Verdict: the exact complement of Ada.
It wins the entire data/metaprogramming column (3, 5, 9) and the ecosystem (10), holds the client half of 1, and has almost none of the safety column (7, 8, most of 6).
The scoreboard pattern is now visible: the ten points split into a design/data column and a safety/contract column, and every candidate picks one.
Eiffel's claim to the list is that it stands closest to the middle.

* Appendix: Dafny in detail

Dafny (Microsoft Research) is a verification language: contracts proven by Z3, compiled to C#, Java, Go or Python.
Point by point:

1. Runtime-checked DbC with blame: the same structural split as Prusti, executed more completely.
   Contracts are the whole language design — pre, post, invariants and termination all proven, and traits enforce behavioral subtyping on overrides (real contract inheritance, checked).
   But it is all static: the 4xx half is missing for the same reason as Prusti — HTTP callers are not Dafny code, so boundary input still needs hand-written validation that you then /prove/ establishes the preconditions.
   Half — the 500 half, done best of anyone.
2. Pipe operator: no.
3. JSON → domain → DB: one twist — Dafny's standard library contains a formally verified JSON serializer (proven correct, not tested).
   YAML, XML and databases: nothing; you reach the host ecosystem via ={:extern}=, unverified.
   No, with an asterisk that matters for 14.
4. Verbs as types with runtime capability query: traits and classes exist; =is= tests are limited and non-idiomatic.
   Half.
5. Metadata channel on fields: ={:attributes}= exist for the compiler, not for user code; no macros to read them.
   No.
6. Null safety: references non-null by default, =T?= opt-in.
   Yes.
7. Compiler-checked concurrency: the language is essentially sequential.
   No.
8. No runtime type holes: fully verified; the soundest candidate possible.
   Yes.
9. Instance-free introspection / derive: no reflection, no metaprogramming; every converter is hand-written /and hand-proven/.
   No.
10. Boring, hardened libraries: no ecosystem of its own; extern to the host language's, across an unverified boundary.
    No.
11. Feature-level generic parameters: =method Decode<T>= is normal Dafny.
    Yes.
12. Schema agreement at compile time: not by derivation but by specification — you write the converter and prove it total over the schema.
    Stronger guarantee than serde's, at perhaps 50× the effort.
    Half, by a different road.
13. Multi-format coexistence: no rule against it, and no formats to coexist.
    Vacuous.
14. Round-trip laws as checkable contracts: the only full 14 on the board.
    =lemma RoundTrip (r) ensures ToRep (ToStore (r)) == r= is a machine-checked theorem, and the standard-library JSON codec ships with exactly such proofs.
    Every other candidate tests this law; Dafny proves it.

Verdict: a proof assistant wearing a programming language's clothes.
Dafny exists to nail the two points RESTLY cannot get anywhere else (the proven 500-half of 1, and all of 14) and offers almost nothing of the remaining twelve.
RESTLY-in-Dafny would be one gloriously verified converter with nothing to serve it.
Its honest role is not candidate but component: write the R ↔ S converter in Dafny, prove the round-trip lemma, compile to C# or Java, and embed it in a host that handles HTTP, formats and the 4xx layer.
Prusti taught us that static and runtime contracts are complements; Dafny sharpens it — verification is a library you link, not a language you live in.

* Appendix: Nim in detail

Nim compiles to C; UFCS syntax, compile-time macros, and custom pragmas are the headline features.
The most interesting candidate yet, because it is strong on exactly the column Eiffel failed.
Point by point:

1. Runtime-checked DbC with blame: not native.
   =assert=/=doAssert= exist; a =contracts= nimble package provides require/ensure/invariant blocks via macros, but it is one-maintainer and there is no blame or contract inheritance.
   Like Rhombus, the macro system could /build/ our tag-grammar DSL — unlike Rhombus, nobody has.
   Half, DIY.
2. Pipe operator: better than a pipe — UFCS.
   =x.f().g()= works for /any/ proc, so every function chains; a literal =|>= is a one-line template if you insist.
   Yes.
3. JSON → domain → DB round-trip: the pieces exist — =std/jsonutils= or =jsony= (generic to/fromJson with hooks), NimYAML, and =norm=, an ORM that generates SQLite DDL from object definitions with pragmas.
   But every piece is one-maintainer polish.
   Half on quality, yes on design.
4. Verbs as types with runtime capability query: ref objects, inheritance, the =of= test, checked downcast.
   Translates directly.
   Yes.
5. Metadata channel on fields: custom pragmas — =order_value {.serializedName: "order".}= readable by macros at compile time.
   First-class.
   Yes.
6. Null safety: value types cannot be nil; ref types can, =strictNotNil= is still experimental, =Option[T]= in std.
   Half.
7. Compiler-checked concurrency: real but partial — ={.gcsafe.}= enforcement, =Isolated[T]= for channel transfer.
   Weaker than Send/Sync, much more than Eiffel gave us.
   Half.
8. No runtime type holes: generics instantiate (no variance holes), variant field access is checked — but =cast= is a casual unchecked escape hatch.
   Yes, with an asterisk.
9. Instance-free introspection at compile time: a crown jewel — =fieldPairs=/=typedesc= iteration over fields is /idiomatic/, macros run at compile time; codec and DDL derivation is exactly how jsony and norm work.
   Yes.
10. Boring, hardened libraries: no.
    The ecosystem is Eiffel-sized; the production HTTP servers (jester, prologue, httpbeast) are community-scale.
    No.
11. Feature-level generic parameters: =proc decode[T](j: JsonNode): T= is everyday Nim.
    Yes.
12. Schema agreement at compile time: yes — a typo'd pragma or type mismatch is a compile error when the deriving macro checks it.
    Yes.
13. Multi-format coexistence: =toJson=/=fromYaml= are just generic procs; no VHPR analogue, one type speaks all formats.
    Yes, though the XML/YAML libraries are thinner than the JSON ones.
14. Round-trip laws as checkable contracts: statable as tests; no standard property-testing story.
    Half.

Verdict: Nim is what Eiffel's data wall wishes it were — compile-time derivation, field metadata, feature-level generics, multi-format — while keeping runtime checks and inheritance for the parts of RESTLY that need them.
Its two failures are precisely Eiffel's: contracts demoted from language to an unmaintained library, and the ecosystem risk of point 10 fully intact.
You would trade ABEL for norm and be praying to a different one-maintainer god.

* Appendix: the Rebol family (Rye, Arturo, Red) in detail

Three descendants of Rebol, evaluated together because they share one trait that settles most of the checklist at once:
code is data, dialects are the core idea, and there is no compile time.
Rebol's genius lands entirely on the wrong side of our interpret-vs-derive line —
these languages /interpret/ schemas more elegantly than anything on the board, and /derive/ nothing, ever.
That is Eiffel's failure mode, dynamic edition,
so the static column (7, 8, 12, the compile-time half of 9) is conceded before scoring starts.

** Rye (ryelang.org) — the standout

Written in Go, embeddable in Go programs.
Point by point, differences from the family baseline:

1. Runtime-checked DbC with blame: half, and an interesting half.
   A /validation dialect/ is built into the core, so boundary validation is explicit and separate from logic — the Pydantic move, the client/4xx half productized.
   No postconditions, no invariants, no contract inheritance: the 500 half does not exist.
2. Pipe operator: the founding syntax idea — op-words and pipe-words are word /types/.
   The best point 2 on the entire board, better than Nim's UFCS; it literally is pipes.
   Yes.
3. JSON → domain → DB: a SQL dialect is built in, JSON is there; no schema layer, so the mismatch ledger is DIY.
   Half.
4. Verbs as types: duck-typed capability checks at runtime, no interfaces.
   Half.
5. Metadata channel: homoiconicity — a schema block is data any dialect can read.
   Yes, at runtime.
6. Null safety: functions never return null; they return a result or a failure value handled in-flow.
   A real null story for a dynamic language, enforced at runtime.
   Half.
7–8. No.
9. Introspection: everything, zero ceremony, runtime only.
   Yes in the Python sense.
10. Boring libraries: no — one maintainer — with an asterisk: Rye embeds in Go, so Go's boring ecosystem is reachable across an unverified glue boundary (Dafny's escape hatch, inverted).
11 + 13. Yes in the vacuous dynamic sense.
12 + 14. No.

Score: 2 + 5 + 9 + 11 + 13, halves of 1, 3, 4, 6.

** Arturo (arturo-lang.io)

The batteries-included one: huge built-in stdlib (JSON and SQLite among the batteries), written in Nim, one maintainer.
No contracts, no validation dialect, null exists.
Score: 5 + 9 + 11 + 13, half of 3 — strictly less than Rye for us.

** Red (red-lang.org)

The ambitious one, and the cautionary tale: still alpha and 32-bit only after fifteen years of chasing the full stack (system programming, reactive GUI, 1 MB binary).
The parse dialect is genuinely great (5, 9, 13), but no pipe, no concurrency, no contracts.
Point 10 fails harder than anything else we have scored: the ecosystem risk is not a column, it is the language itself.
Score: 5 + 9 + 11 + 13.

** Verdict

Nobody in the family stacks better than what we already have.
The comparison that settles it: Rhombus dominates the family at its own game —
same design column (2 via user-defined operators, 5 and 9 via macros, 13),
plus the one thing no Rebol descendant has: a real contract system with blame, which is RESTLY's thesis,
and its macros run at compile time, giving a whiff of derivation the family structurally cannot reach.
Rye earns a footnote for the best pipe story and the only built-in validation dialect we have seen anywhere;
like Dafny, its honest role is component (Go glue and config layer), not candidate.

* Appendix: Kotlin in detail

Kotlin was on the scoreboard from the first pass; the detailed treatment recounted its row upward.
Point by point:

1. Runtime-checked DbC with blame: no contracts, but a fossil of our thesis hides in the stdlib —
   =require()= throws =IllegalArgumentException= (client's fault) and =check()= throws =IllegalStateException= (supplier's fault).
   That is blame vocabulary, and every Spring/Ktor app maps IAE → 400 by folk tradition.
   But there are no postconditions, no invariants, no contract inheritance, no labels;
   =kotlin.contracts= is smart-cast hints for the compiler, not DbC.
   No, with the require/check asterisk.
2. Pipe operator: no =|>=; extension functions and =let=/=run= give chaining that covers most of the need.
   Half.
3. JSON → domain → DB round-trip: kotlinx.serialization is a compiler plugin —
   =@SerialName= (rename), =@Transient= (skip), custom serializers (coerce): the mismatch ledger on the field.
   Exposed and SQLDelight generate the DDL.
   Yes.
4. Verbs as types with runtime capability query: smart casts make it the /idiom/ —
   =if (storage is Postable)= and =storage= just /is/ Postable inside the branch, cleaner than our =attached= locals.
   Yes, the best ergonomics on the board.
5. Metadata channel on fields: annotations, readable at compile time by KSP and plugins, at runtime by reflection.
   Yes.
6. Null safety: the language's founding feature.
   Yes.
7. Compiler-checked concurrency: no — coroutines are ergonomics, not race checking; shared mutable state compiles happily.
   No.
8. No runtime type holes: split verdict — declaration-site variance (=in=/=out=) is checked, so Eiffel's catcall is impossible,
   but JVM erasure punches its own holes (=List<String>= casts unchecked).
   Half.
9. Instance-free compile-time introspection: KSP and the serialization plugin derive from declarations at compile time, no instance needed.
   Yes — the original scoreboard row undersold this.
10. Boring, hardened libraries: the JVM — Jackson, JDBC, Ktor, Spring, decades of dull.
    Tied with Python for best on the board.
    Yes.
11. Feature-level generic parameters: =fun <T> decode(s: String): T=,
    plus =reified= type parameters that survive erasure at inline call sites: =Json.decodeFromString<Todo>(s)=.
    Yes.
12. Schema agreement at compile time: the serialization plugin derives codecs from the class;
    type mismatches are compile errors (string-typed =@SerialName= typos are not).
    Yes.
13. Multi-format coexistence: textbook point 13 — =Json=, =Cbor=, =Yaml= are /values/ you pass,
    one =@Serializable= class speaks all of them.
    The sentence "format = plugged-in value, not inheritance decision" implemented literally.
    Yes.
14. Round-trip laws: kotest property testing; tested, not proven, same as Rust.
    Half.

Score: 3 + 4 + 5 + 6 + 9 + 10 + 11 + 12 + 13, halves of 2, 8 and 14 — the widest coverage on the board, wider than Nim because it keeps 10.

Verdict: Kotlin is the boring answer — Nim's entire data column plus the ecosystem Nim prays for, losing exactly one thing: the contract thesis.
Point 1 collapses to =require()=-throws-IAE,
a folk RESTLY that productized the 4xx half by accident and abandoned the 500 half entirely.
RESTLY-in-Kotlin ships fastest and proves nothing;
the part of the codebase we are proudest of becomes a convention, not a contract.

* Appendix: D in detail

D looked, on the spec sheet, like the unicorn: the only language designed with both of our load-bearing walls in it.
The detailed look demoted it.
Point by point:

1. Runtime-checked DbC with blame: in the spec, everything we want —
   =in=/=out= contract blocks and =invariant= on classes, runtime-checked,
   pre-vs-post giving Eiffel-grade blame,
   and real contract inheritance (overrides or-combine =in= contracts and and-combine =out= contracts: =require else=/=ensure then= semantics, one of only three languages on the board with it).
   In the culture, dead:
   the HOPL paper /Origins of the D Programming Language/ counts contract programming among the features that did not live up to expectations;
   Phobos itself barely uses =in=/=out=;
   the community writes plain =assert= at the top of function bodies, which for preconditions is nearly equivalent;
   DIP 1009's terse =in (expr)= syntax was motivated by the block syntax being too verbose for anyone to bother with;
   and contract inheritance spent years buggy because so little real code exercised it.
   A contract system nobody uses is unmaintained masonry — nobody files the bugs, nobody writes the blame tooling.
   Half: technically present, culturally dead.
2. Pipe operator: no =|>=; UFCS gives =x.f.g= chaining for free functions.
   Half.
3. JSON → domain → DB round-trip: mir-ion, asdf and vibe.d serialization all derive codecs from struct definitions.
   Yes.
4. Verbs as types with runtime capability query: interfaces and checked casts.
   Yes.
5. Metadata channel on fields: UDAs (User Defined Attributes) — =@serdeKeys("order") long orderValue;= — the mismatch ledger on the field.
   Yes.
6. Null safety: none; references are nullable and nothing checks.
   The category of bugs Eiffel deleted for us comes back.
   No.
7. Compiler-checked concurrency: =shared= is a qualifier, not a proof.
   Half.
8. No runtime type holes: the =@safe= subset exists but casts escape it.
   Half.
9. Instance-free compile-time introspection: arguably best in class anywhere —
   =__traits(allMembers)=, =static foreach=, CTFE;
   deriving a codec /and our DDL/ from a struct is idiomatic library code.
   Yes.
10. Boring, hardened libraries: the familiar curse — three compilers, Phobos v2/v3 drama, vibe.d at community scale.
    Bigger than Eiffel's ecosystem, far from boring.
    No.
11. Feature-level generic parameters: templates — =T decode(T)(string json)=.
    Yes.
12. Schema agreement at compile time: derivation is compile-time; a UDA-checked deriver rejects mismatches as compile errors.
    Yes.
13. Multi-format coexistence: formats are libraries, no VHPR analogue; one struct speaks JSON, ion, msgpack.
    Yes.
14. Round-trip laws: built-in =unittest= blocks make the law cheap to state; tested, not proven.
    Half.

Score: 3 + 4 + 5 + 9 + 11 + 12 + 13, halves of 1, 2, 7, 8 and 14; misses 6 and 10 — Nim's twin, not the unicorn.

Verdict: D is the third member of a graveyard that proves our question has been asked before.
C# Code Contracts died as a library (runtime DbC with inheritance on top of C#'s full data column, abandoned ~2015);
Cobra died as a language (Python syntax with native require/ensure/invariant, abandoned ~2013);
D's contracts died inside a living language.
Three independent burials of exactly the combination this checklist asks for.
The survivors of "contracts + data column" are the languages where contracts are load-bearing for something:
Eiffel (the culture), Ada (certification), Dafny (the proofs).
The lesson generalizes point 10 from libraries to language features:
a feature is only as real as the community that keeps it honest.
